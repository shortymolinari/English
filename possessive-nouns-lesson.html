How to show that something belongs to someone — and where to put that tiny apostrophe

00

What Is a Possessive Noun?

A possessive noun shows that something belongs to or is associated with a person, animal or thing. In English, we show this with an apostrophe ( ' ).

In Spanish we use "de" — in English we flip it around and use an apostrophe:

Spanish — uses DE
el carro de María
el libro del estudiante
la casa de mis padres
English — uses 's
María's car
the student's book
my parents' house
Singular Possessive
noun + 's
one owner → add apostrophe + s
→
the dog's tail  — la cola del perro
→
Sofia's phone  — el teléfono de Sofía
→
my mother's birthday  — el cumpleaños de mi mamá
→
the company's policy  — la política de la empresa
→
Colombia's capital  — la capital de Colombia
Plural Possessive
nouns'
multiple owners → apostrophe after the s
→
my parents' house  — la casa de mis padres
→
the students' results  — los resultados de los estudiantes
→

→

→

01

Singular Possessive — noun + 's

's
One owner → add apostrophe + s
Works for people, animals, places and things. The owner goes first.
singular noun + ' + s + [owned thing]
Carlos + car
Carlos's car
Carlos's car is blue.
El carro de Carlos es azul.
the teacher + desk
the teacher's desk
The teacher's desk is messy.
El escritorio del profesor está desordenado.
my sister + room
my sister's room
My sister's room is always clean.
El cuarto de mi hermana siempre está limpio.
the cat + tail
the cat's tail
The cat's tail is very fluffy.
La cola del gato es muy esponjosa.
the company + logo
the company's logo
The company's logo changed last year.
El logo de la empresa cambió el año pasado.
Medellín + metro
Medellín's metro
Medellín's metro is the best in Colombia!
El metro de Medellín es el mejor de Colombia.
02

Plural Possessive — nouns' (after the s)

s'
Multiple owners → apostrophe goes AFTER the existing s
The noun is already plural (ends in -s). Just add the apostrophe at the end — no extra s needed.
plural noun (ends in s) + ' + [owned thing]
my parents (plural) + house
my parents' house
My parents' house is near the park.
La casa de mis padres está cerca del parque.
the students (plural) + books
the students' books
I collected all the students' books.
Recogí todos los libros de los estudiantes.
my friends (plural) + party
my friends' party
My friends' party was incredible!
¡La fiesta de mis amigos fue increíble!
the employees + salaries
the employees' salaries
The employees' salaries increased this year.
Los salarios de los empleados aumentaron este año.
the girls (plural) + team
the girls' team
The girls' team won the championship!
¡El equipo de las niñas ganó el campeonato!
my neighbors + dogs
my neighbors' dogs
My neighbors' dogs bark all night.
Los perros de mis vecinos ladran toda la noche.
03

Irregular Plural Possessives — Special Rule!

+
Irregular plurals don't end in -s → treat them like singulars: add 's
Words like children, men, women, people — their plural doesn't end in -s, so they take apostrophe + s just like a singular noun.
irregular plural + ' + s + [owned thing]
children (not childrens)
children's toys
The children's toys are everywhere!
Los juguetes de los niños están por todas partes.
men (not mens)
men's clothing
The men's section is on the 2nd floor.
La sección de hombres está en el 2do piso.
women (not womens)
women's rights
Women's rights have advanced a lot.
Los derechos de la mujer han avanzado mucho.
people (not peoples)
people's opinions
People's opinions on this topic vary widely.
Las opiniones de la gente sobre esto varían mucho.
04

Names Ending in S — James's or James'?

When a person's name already ends in -s (like James, Carlos, Lucas, Chris), there are two accepted styles. Both are correct — just be consistent:

Style 1 — add 's (more common, spoken)
Style 2 — add ' only (formal / written)
James's car — sounds: "James-iz"
Carlos's jacket
Chris's idea
Lucas's phone
James' car — also accepted
Carlos' jacket
Chris' idea
Lucas' phone
💡 Tip In everyday spoken English and informal writing, James's is more common and natural. James' is preferred in formal and journalistic writing. Pick one style and stick with it.
05

The "OF" Alternative — When to use it

English also uses "of" to show possession — similar to Spanish "de". But the two structures are NOT always interchangeable. Here's when to use each:

Context 's (people & animals) of (things & ideas)
── People & Animates ──
People María's house✓ Natural — use 's for people the house of María ✗ (sounds odd)
Animals the dog's owner✓ Natural for animals the owner of the dog (also OK)
── Things & Abstract Concepts ──
Objects the table's leg ✗ (unnatural) the leg of the table✓ Use "of" for things
Buildings the museum's entrance (acceptable) the entrance of the museum✓ Preferred for buildings
Abstract ideas — not used — the end of the movie✓ Concepts, parts, ends
── Organizations & Places (both OK) ──
Companies Google's new product✓ Very common in news/media the new product of Google✓ Also correct
Countries Colombia's culture✓ Common in journalism the culture of Colombia✓ More formal
06

Possessive vs Plural — Don't Mix Them Up!

One of the most common mistakes in English — even native speakers get this wrong. The apostrophe is ONLY for possession, never for making a word plural.

Possessive (ownership) Plural (more than one) Possessive Plural
the cat's food
la comida del gato (1 gato)
three cats
tres gatos — NO apostrophe!
the cats' food
la comida de los gatos (varios)
my brother's job
el trabajo de mi hermano
two brothers
dos hermanos — NO apostrophe!
my brothers' jobs
los trabajos de mis hermanos
the doctor's office
el consultorio del médico
five doctors
cinco médicos — NO apostrophe!
the doctors' meeting
la reunión de los médicos
a child's toy
el juguete de un niño
many children
muchos niños — NO apostrophe!
children's toys
los juguetes de los niños (irregular)

Example Sentences

Singular 's — person
I borrowed my roommate's umbrella — I hope she doesn't mind!
Tomé prestado el paraguas de mi compañera de cuarto — ¡espero que no le importe!
Plural s' — multiple owners
The teachers' meeting was postponed to next week.
La reunión de los profesores fue aplazada para la próxima semana.
Singular 's — animal
The dog's collar fell off during our walk in the park.
El collar del perro se cayó durante nuestro paseo en el parque.
Irregular plural — children's
The children's laughter filled the whole house on Christmas morning.
La risa de los niños llenó toda la casa la mañana de Navidad.
"of" construction — thing
I couldn't believe the ending of the movie — completely unexpected!
No podía creer el final de la película — ¡completamente inesperado!
Mixed — 's + plural s'
I parked next to my boss's car, right between my colleagues' bikes.
Parqué al lado del carro de mi jefe, justo entre las bicicletas de mis colegas.
Singular 's — place / country
Colombia's biodiversity is among the highest in the world.
La biodiversidad de Colombia está entre las más altas del mundo.
Plural s' — group / team
The players' performance in the second half was outstanding.
El rendimiento de los jugadores en el segundo tiempo fue sobresaliente.

📌 Key Notes — Possessive Nouns

  • Singular → add 's: María's car · the cat's tail · Colombia's culture
  • Regular plural (ends in -s) → add ' only: my parents' house · the students' books
  • Irregular plural (doesn't end in -s) → add 's like a singular: children's toys · men's shoes · women's section
  • Names ending in -s: both James's and James' are accepted — pick one style.
  • Use 's for people and animals. Use of for objects, abstract ideas and parts: the end of the movie · the leg of the table
  • The apostrophe is NEVER used to make a word plural. apple's ✗ for "several apples" — use apples ✓
  • Owner goes first in English: María's car (not "the car of María" for people).

⚠ Most common mistakes:

✗ three apple's    ✓ three apples (plural has NO apostrophe!)

✗ the childrens' room    ✓ the children's room (irregular plural → treat like singular)

✗ my parent's house (if you have two parents)    ✓ my parents' house

✗ the cat's are hungry    ✓ the cats are hungry (just plural, no possession here)

✗ the leg's of the table    ✓ the legs of the table (use "of" for things)
